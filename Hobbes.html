Thomas Hobbes

Foundations of Modern Political Thought

This interactive guide covers the foundational theories of Thomas Hobbes as presented in his 1651 work, Leviathan.

Hobbes attempts to create a "social science" by applying the principles of the scientific revolution—specifically geometry and the laws of motion—to human behavior. He argues that humans are self-interested, material beings who, in the absence of a central authority, would exist in a state of perpetual war. To escape this, individuals enter into a social contract to create an absolute sovereign.

1. Methodology & Materialism

Hobbes built his political theory on a strict scientific foundation, rejecting traditional spiritual explanations of human behavior.

🔬

Resolutive-Compositive Method

Inspired by Galileo, Hobbes breaks society down into its smallest parts (individuals and their biology) and then rebuilds it to understand how the whole functions. You cannot understand the state without first dissecting the mechanics of the human mind.

🧱

Materialism vs. Dualism

Hobbes firmly rejected the Cartesian idea that the soul and body are separate. He believed humans are entirely physical systems governed by the laws of motion. Even complex thoughts and emotions are simply "internal motions" reacting to external stimuli.

2. Human Psychology: Calculating The Will

Hobbes argued for Psychological Egoism: the belief that all human actions are motivated by self-interest. He reduced human motivation to a mechanical calculation of forces.

3. The State of Nature

A hypothetical condition without government. Hobbes asks us to imagine humanity stripped of all artificial laws.

⚖️

The Irony of Equality

Hobbes makes a radical claim: human beings are naturally equal. However, this equality leads to war, not peace. Why?

Because if two people want the same scarce resource, and are roughly equal in their physical or intellectual ability to kill one another, they live in constant fear. They must strike first to ensure their own survival.

4. The Leviathan & Social Contract

To escape the brutal State of Nature, rational humans agree to a radical solution: surrendering their absolute freedom for absolute security.

Key Detail: Absolutism for Peace

Notice that the contract is among the people, not between the people and the ruler. Because the Sovereign didn't sign the contract, they cannot legally break it. Hobbes justifies total sovereign power (including censorship) not by "Divine Right," but because divided power inevitably leads back to civil war. The obligation to obey only ends if the Sovereign can no longer provide protection.
