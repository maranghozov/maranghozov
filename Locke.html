Equality, Freedom, and the Foundation of Constitutional Monarchy

The transition of John Locke’s thought from early authoritarian leanings to becoming the "father of liberalism."

This guide explores John Locke’s monumental refutation of absolute monarchy (targeting Robert Filmer's Patriarchalism) and his revolutionary ideas regarding property.

Locke posited that legitimate government must be strictly based on the consent of the governed. Its primary purpose is not to rule absolutely, but to protect the natural rights of its citizens: life, liberty, and estate.

💡 The Core Argument

Locke provided the intellectual justification for overturning a ruler who violates the trust of the people, cementing the shift of power from the Crown to Parliament during the Glorious Revolution.

Historical Timeline

Explore the turbulent reigns that shaped Locke's political radicalization. Click on each era to reveal details.

Context: Monarchy is restored. Locke enters Oxford to study and teach after living through the Civil War.

Locke's Role (1666): Meets Anthony Ashley Cooper (Earl of Shaftesbury). Becomes his personal physician and advisor, thrusting Locke into London politics.

The Conflict & Exclusion Crisis: Shaftesbury opposes Charles II, fearing Catholic succession (James) and absolute monarchy. Locke aids the "exclusion" campaign to bar James from the throne, marking his shift to active political resistance.

Exile (1683): The exclusion campaign fails. Shaftesbury is accused of treason. Locke flees to Holland for safety.

Radicalization: In Holland, Locke associates with English rebels plotting against James II.

Two Treatises: Written roughly between 1679-1683, Locke crafts the justification for overturning rulers who violate the people's trust. James II rapidly alienates his supporters.

The Glorious Revolution (1688): William is invited to take the throne. James II flees to France in a "bloodless" shift of power.

Return (1689): Locke returns to England accompanying Princess Mary on the royal yacht.

Intellectual Justification: Locke publishes Two Treatises of Government and Letter Concerning Toleration. They become the definitive justification for the new regime and parliamentary supremacy.

State of Nature: A Philosophical Comparison

Thomas Hobbes John Locke
⚖

A State of Peace & Reason

Humans are naturally free and equal. The state of nature is governed by reason. Conflict is not inevitable, but arises due to "inconveniences"—mainly the lack of an unbiased judge to settle property disputes. Founded on an assumption of Abundance.

⚔

War of All Against All

Human life is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." Humans are driven by fear and competition. A strong, absolute sovereign is required to prevent continuous violent conflict. Founded on an assumption of Scarcity.

The Labor Theory of Property

Locke famously claimed that mixing one's labor with natural resources creates private property. He estimated that the vast majority of a product's value is derived from human industry, not the raw materials.

The Spoilage Proviso

One can only claim as much property from nature as they can use before it spoils.

The Role of Money

The invention of gold/silver bypasses the spoilage limit. Because money does not decay, wealth can be accumulated indefinitely without violating natural law or injuring others.

The Analogy: Lions vs. Foxes

Locke uses this powerful analogy to critique the logic of absolute monarchy. Why would reasonable people protect themselves from minor threats while exposing themselves to a massive one?

🦊

The Foxes

Represent thieving neighbors or minor criminals in the state of nature. People create civil society to protect themselves from these common threats.

➡️
🦁

The Lions

Represent Absolute Monarchs. Locke argues it is foolish to build defenses against foxes only to hand all power over to a lion who is completely above the law.
